SWT2 - Emergency Shut Down (ESD)

ESD Summary

    Here’s a summary of the "SWT - Emergency Shut Down (ESD)" video in 8 bullet points with timestamps:
  1. (00:00 – 00:46): The Emergency Shutdown (ESD) system controls the flowline valve actuator and possibly a surface safety valve (SSV). It can be triggered from push-button stations at various locations like the separator, steam exchanger, gauge tank, and burner pedestals.
  2. (01:24 – 02:08): The ESD control skid contains:
    • A pump
    • Hydraulic tank
    • Air reservoir
    • Three hose reels (1x 20m high-pressure, 2x 90m low-pressure) It works with any single-action fail-safe actuator (≤6000 psi).
  3. (02:08 – 02:52): The system allows for remote emergency closure of the flowline valve during events like fire, pipe leaks, or equipment malfunction using a pneumatic console, manual push-pull stations, and pressure pilots.
  4. (02:52 – 03:24): High-pressure pilot logic: If pressure exceeds a preset level, it vents air, triggering the interface valve to bleed hydraulic pressure, causing the shutdown valve to close.
  5. (03:24 – 04:06): Low-pressure pilot logic: If pressure drops below a preset level, it vents air, prompting the system to close the shutdown valve similarly.
  6. (04:06 – 05:09): The ESD system includes:
    • Hydraulic valves
    • Flow head actuator
    • Surface safety valve
    • High/low pilots (30 psi)
    • Panel components like hydraulic pump, oil tank, air vessel (150 psi), interface valve, and various control valves.
  7. (05:09 – 06:10): ESD stations must be located at the separator, rig floor, escape routes, and near the lab cabin. Pilots should be placed on the flowline, data header, test separator, and steam exchanger to ensure full system coverage.

Q&A Summary

    Here’s a summary of ESD in oil wells
  1. What is the primary function of the Emergency Shutdown (ESD) system?

    Answer: The ESD system is designed to **quickly close the well in emergency situations** such as fire, pipe break, or separator malfunction to ensure safety.

  2. What components does the ESD control skid include?

    Answer: It includes a hydraulic pump, hydraulic oil tank, air reservoir, and three hose reels (one high-pressure, two low-pressure).

  3. What type of actuator does the ESD system support?

    Answer: It supports single-action fail-safe hydraulic actuators with hydraulic pressure requirements not exceeding 6000 psi.

  4. Where can ESD push-button stations be installed?

    Answer: The high-pressure pilot is triggered when flowline pressure rises above a preset threshold, indicating potential issues like choke plugging.

  5. What triggers the high-pressure pilot in the ESD system?

    Answer: The high-pressure pilot is triggered when flowline pressure rises above a preset threshold, indicating potential issues like choke plugging.

  6. What happens when the high or low pilot is triggered?

    Answer: When triggered, the air pressure is vented, the interface valve releases hydraulic pressure, and the shutdown valve closes.

  7. What pressure is typically used by the high/low pilots and ESD stations?

    Answer: Both the high/low pilots and ESD stations typically operate at 30 psi.

  8. What is the role of the interface valve in the ESD system?

    Answer: The interface valve controls the release of hydraulic pressure from the actuator when triggered by pilot signals or manual stations, leading to valve closure.

  9. What is the function of the surface safety valve (SSV) in the ESD system?

    Answer: The SSV is an optional component located upstream of the choke manifold and helps isolate the well during an emergency.

  10. What equipment makes up the ESD panel?

    Answer: The ESD panel includes:

    • Hydraulic pump
    • Hydraulic oil tank
    • Air vessel (150 psi)
    • Interface valve
    • Check valve
    • Quick exhaust valve
    • Reset valve
    • Bypass valve
