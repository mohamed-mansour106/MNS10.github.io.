Transfer pump Summary

    Here‚Äôs a summarized version of the video ‚ÄúTransfer pump‚Äù based on the transcript, in 5‚Äì10 bullet points with timestamps:
  1. (00:00) Purpose: Learn about different types of transfer pumps used in well testing setups; these pumps move fluids from tanks to oil manifolds.
  2. (00:44) Positive Displacement Pumps:
    • Types: rotary/screw, diaphragm, and reciprocating.
    • Ideal for high viscosity fluids, accurate metering, and high-pressure applications.
    • Benefits: High efficiency, low driver power, adjustable flow rate, low shear.
    • Drawbacks: Sensitive to dry running, needs pressure relief valve, causes pulsation, limited flow range.
  3. (01:21) Centrifugal Pumps:
    • Use a rotating impeller to create velocity and pressure.
    • Types: axial and radial flow.
    • Pros: Simple design, low maintenance, available in many sizes, good for large flow rates.
    • Cons: Less effective with viscous fluids and high system back pressure.
  4. (03:05) Screw Pump:
    • Positive displacement type; moves fluid through screw-thread-like design.
    • High efficiency, especially with viscous fluids; does not generate pressure, only moves fluid.
  5. (03:35) Diaphragm Pump:
    • Uses reciprocating diaphragm and check valves.
    • Variants include air-operated versions.
    • Features: Low capacity and pressure, no seals, high pulsation.
  6. (04:53) Gear Pump:
    • Rotary positive displacement type using interlocking gears.
    • Moves fluid through teeth cavities; two designs: internal and external.
  7. (05:31) Centrifugal Pump (again):
    • Advantages: Cost-effective, simple, fewer parts, flexible size options.
  8. (06:07) Real-World Examples:
    • Viking Pump: Rotary, diesel/electric, for low viscosity, non-H2S.
    • Mono Pump: Screw type, handles all viscosities and H2S.
    • Sundyne Pump: Centrifugal, electric, handles impurities and H2S.
    • Gould‚Äôs Pump: Centrifugal, H2S-proof.
