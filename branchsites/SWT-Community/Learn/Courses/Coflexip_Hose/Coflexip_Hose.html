Lesson 2

Material Anatomy: Core & Inner Liners

Coflexip hoses present high resistance to corrosion and minimal fluid restrictions because they lack in-line elbows. They are composed structurally of non-bonded layers of stainless steel and high-grade thermoplastic materials.

7.1

Thermoplastic Inner Liner

The primary layer that makes the pipeline entirely leak-proof. This layer determines the upper service temperature constraints and direct chemical compatibility margins. It can be further reinforced with integrated aramid tapes.

7.2

Interlocked Zeta Layer

Absorbs the massive hoop stress induced by internal pressure parameters and external crushing loads. Critical Engineering Fact: It is the precise mechanical interlocking design of this Zeta layer that limits the Minimum Bending Radius (MBR) of the pipeline.

7.3 Metallic Reinforcement

Zeta Structural Reinforcement

Utilized exclusively under extreme high-pressure scenarios. It incorporates a flat, non-interlocked high-tensile steel layer added over the standard Zeta mechanism.

7.4 Intermediate Sheath

Anti-Friction Thermoplastic Sheath

A thin, advanced layer designed explicitly to optimize dynamic behavior and friction indices during operation. Note: This layer is completely non-leak-proof.

Lesson 3

Shielding & External Protection

7.5

Double Cross-Wound Steel Armour

Engineered specifically to resist severe axial loads generated by extreme internal operating pressures or external tensile pulling forces. This layer also provides the essential resistance required to counter destructive torque forces.

7.6

Thermoplastic Outer Sheath

A fully leak-proof layer designed to perform two vital protection roles:
a) Protect inner steel armour wires from corrosion.
b) Guard the structure against hydrostatic external crushing forces when deployed in subsea configurations.

7.7

Fire Resistant Layer (Special Order)

A critical addition for choke and kill lines on fixed platforms. Provides continuous structural survival and full working pressure containment capabilities at temperatures up to 700°C (1,292°F) for a duration well exceeding 30 minutes.

7.8

Stainless Steel Outer Wrap (Carcass)

Provides a rugged external mechanical shield safeguarding the inner thermoplastic outer sheath from harsh environments (impacts, severe friction, cuts). It does not directly add to internal pressure containment.

Lesson 4

Minimum Bending Radius (MBR) Physics

Exceeding the Minimum Bending Radius (MBR) introduces irreversible mechanical degradation. The interlocked Zeta layer will separate, removing the structural support of the inner thermoplastic liner, resulting in catastrophic perforation when subjected to operating pressure.

Standard Oilfield Rule of Thumb
$$MBR = 12 \times ID$$

Where ID represents the true Inner Diameter of the flexible steel pipeline.

Rigging & Mishandling Risk Profile

Over-bending typically happens during handling and initial deck deployment operations. Critical priority inspection must focus on the first meter (few feet) of flexible line immediately adjacent to the rigid end terminations.

Lesson 5

Routing & Integrity Verification

Preferred operational installation follows a strict "J" or "U" configuration profile with the hard-end fittings pointed upward in a true vertical orientation. If platform constraints prohibit vertical paths, non-vertical layouts must be secured to avoid over-bending at the end fitting.

Field Inspection & Troubleshooting Scenarios

Scenario 1: Water Seepage from the Outer Carcass Wrap +

The Situation: During a rainstorm, water is observed slowly seeping out from the joints of the stainless steel outer wrap carcass.

Engineering Standard Protocol:

Water seeping from the outer carcass does not mean structural damage. Because this outer wrap is completely non-bonded, it naturally expands slightly, allowing trapped ambient fluids (like rainwater) to drain away from the inner thermoplastic sheath.

Scenario 2: Suspected Fluid Leak / Continuous Core Seepage +

The Situation: Continuous fluid seepage is observed from the hose during pressurized backflow operations, raising doubts regarding inner liner integrity.

Mandatory Action Steps:

  1. Isolate the hose immediately (declare out of service).
  2. Bleed down internal pressure safely.
  3. Pick up the line, fill it completely with fresh water, and allow any residual trapped exterior water to completely drain away.
  4. Observe closely to check if the internal water level drops.
  5. If doubts remain, perform a hydrostatic test to Cold Working Pressure (CWP) under extreme safety conditions.
Lesson 6

HPHT Materials & Sour Service Metallurgy

Standard flexible lines utilize Rislan for internal linings. However, true High-Pressure/High-Temperature (HPHT) applications demand an architectural shift to Coflon—a specialized fluorinated thermoplastic known as Polyvinylidene Fluoride (PVDF). This material shift changes the line's thermal limits and chemical exposure thresholds.

Coflon Operational Boundary Matrix (CO₂ + H₂S Limits)

Maximum Pressure Maximum Temperature Fluid Condition Limit (CO₂ + H₂S Combined)
15,000 psi (1,035 bar) 266°F (130°C) < 10% Concentration
10,000 psi (690 bar) 266°F (130°C) < 30% Concentration
10,000 psi (690 bar) 230°F (110°C) < 50% Concentration

Sour Service Compatibility (NACE MR-01-75 / ISO 15156)

All internal metallic end fittings and flexible pipe layers coming into direct contact with production fluids comply strictly with the NACE MR-01-75 (2001 Edition) standard.

Operational Warning: Due to molecular gas diffusion, sour gas components will slowly permeate through the thermoplastic inner liner over time. Consequently, structural carbon steel elements—including the Interlocked Zeta layer, reinforcement wires, and cross-wound armours—are constructed from specialty steels pre-tested against Hydrogen-Induced Cracking (HIC) via TM0284 and Sulfide Stress Cracking (SSC) via TM0177 protocols.

Lesson 7

Pre-Rig-Up Operational Readiness

Rigging up flexible lines between a marine vessel and a Wellhead Platform or Tower is a high-risk operation. The pre-job phase requires formal alignment across marine, crane, and well testing teams.

1. Logistics & Weather Windows

  • Obtain and review an official minimum 48-hour localized weather and sea-state forecast.
  • Enforce an exclusion zone: All non-essential personnel must remain entirely clear of the hose deployment path at all times.
  • Ensure an approved Permit to Work (PTW) is active, verified, and posted at the control station.

2. Hardware Rigging Safety

  • Verify that one end of the Coflexip line is pre-connected to the vessel's Hydraulic Quick Disconnect Coupling (HQDC).
  • Confirm that the HQDC nitrogen/hydraulic accumulators are pre-charged to standard operating specs.
  • Inspect all lifting slings, taglines, and securing platform shackles. Secure the Hose Hanger Adaptor (HHA) clamp to the tower side of the hose using dual-legged slings.

Field Command Architecture during Emergency Disconnect

Roles must be explicitly distinct to avoid dropped objects, crushed lines, or unexpected structural tension:

Well Test Supervisor Maintains cross-party radio comms and holds single-point emergency disconnect authority.
Banksman / Signalman Maintains dedicated line-of-sight visual control with the Marine Vessel Crane Operator.
Rigging Crew Lead Directly supervises manual sling engagement and physical line hook/unhook cycles.
Lesson 8

Marine Vessel to Tower Rig-Up

The mechanical execution sequence must be carried out precisely to safeguard structural assets and prevent kinks or over-bending near connection points.

01
Vessel Mooring & Stabilization Marine crew heaves up anchors and maneuvers the vessel close to the platform structure following explicit client protocols. The bow wire is securely transferred and attached to the tower's mooring buoy.
02
Depressurization & Line Isolation Before connecting fittings, confirm that both main ball valves at the tower's boat landing are fully closed with protection caps secure. Bleed off any residual downstream pressure to 0 psi through the needle valves.
03
Rigging Cross-Overs & Chicksans Hoist the Double Sheave Rope Block (DSRB), high-pressure chicksans, cross-overs, and structural flanges to the tower deck. Assemble and hammer up connections to both the Test line and the Re-injection lines.
04
Process Production Test Hose Transfer Lift the primary process production test hose via the crane. Secure it to the DSRB using a rated shackle at the correct elevation, then tie it off directly to the tower bollard before hammering the wing unions home.
05
Re-injection Line Completion Release the crane from the test hose, pick up the secondary Re-injection line, and repeat the rigging sequence to connect it to the tower's return flow infrastructure.
CRITICAL FIELD NOTE: Throughout the entire lifting and securement sequence, the opposite ends of both hoses must remain rigidly locked and anchored into the vessel's onboard onboard process system through the Hydraulic Quick Disconnect Coupling (HQDC).
Lesson 9

Hydrotesting & Flow Line-Up

Once the rigging is locked in, the entire system must undergo a staged hydrostatic pressure validation to confirm connection sealing integrity before routing live well hydrocarbons.

Phase A: System Priming & Low Pressure Equalization

Verify that all hammer unions, Greylock clamps, and structural flanges are tight. Line up the low-pressure system and start the Sulzer Sea Water Pump to prime and boost the high-pressure Oil Well Pump. Line up the air compressor supply to engage the high-pressure unit's pneumatic starter system.

Phase B: Staged Staged Pressure Management

  • Stage 1 (Return/Re-injection Line): Pressure up the return line and joints slowly to 1,500 psi. This lower limit accounts for the "bottleneck effect" on lower Cold Working Pressure (CWP) rated downstream piping. Inspect thoroughly for visible leaks.
  • Stage 2 (Primary Test Line): If the first stage is stable, isolate the equalizer manifold and step up the primary test line pressure to 3,000 psi. Hold this pressure for a minimum 15-minute verification window.

Phase C: Bleed Down & Diverting Live Well Production

Once the verification hold finishes with zero drop, open the bleeder manifold valve to the sea, reducing water system pressure to exactly 0 psig. Secure the pump and compressor. The tower operator can then safely line up the production separator, open the tower process valves, and gradually close the main block valve to route live well flow through the flexible system.

Lesson 10

Well Diverting & Live Flow Maneuvers

Diverting live hydrocarbon flow from a rigid Wellhead Tower infrastructure through a flexible temporary test configuration requires precise coordination between the Tower Operator and the onboard Well Test Supervisor.

Step 1: Communication & Equalization

The Tower Operator notes the initial static/flowing Wellhead Pressure (WHP) and communicates it via radio to the Supervisor. Upon direct authorization, the operator opens the tower's Re-injection Valve to establish a downstream path.

Step 2: Separator Pressure Optimization

To ensure fluids move efficiently without back-flow or blockages, the test separator's operating pressure must be set higher than the sea line/production line pressure. This differential allows oil and gas to be pushed smoothly through the re-injection point.

Complete Diversion Verification: Once flow stabilizes through the test vessel, the Tower Operator closes the main Production Diverter Valve on the tower. Hydrocarbons are now fully routed through the test separator and returned via the re-injection line.

Lesson 11

Rig-Down Prep & Purge Workflows

Safely decommissioning temporary flow lines requires removing all trapped hazardous hydrocarbons. The system must be fully depressurized, isolated, and flushed before breaking any physical connections.

The Hydrocarbon Bleed-Off & Flushing Protocol

1

Well Realignment & Isolation: The Tower Operator redirects the well back to the main production header. The test line and re-injection valves on the tower are closed to isolate the flexible hoses from the reservoir.

2

Vessel System Line-Up: The separator oil outlet is closed, the orifice plate is pulled from its housing, and the gas line is lined up to the burner boom. The Back Pressure Control Valve (BPCV) is kept closed to manage the flow manually.

3

Flaring & Depressurization: Ensure propane pilots are lit and the wind direction favors flaring. Slowly adjust the manual gas valves to drop the system pressure down to 0 psig, venting remaining gas through the burner heads.

4

Air Forced Flush: With the system at zero, engage the air compressor to flush the entire system with air, sweeping out trace gases. Once clear, extinguish the propane pilots, close the choke manifold, and shut the vessel re-injection valve.

Lesson 12

Mechanical Rig-Down & Coiling

Once fluid isolation and atmospheric depressurization are verified, the physical rig-down of the heavy lines can begin. This process follows a structured sequence to avoid damaging the lines' internal layers.

1. Component Disconnection

Disconnect the lines one at a time, starting with the re-injection hose. The marine crane hooks into the lifting belt to take the full weight of the line, allowing the tower crew to slack off and un-shackle the Double Sheave Rope Block (DSRB).

2. Controlled Lowering & On-Deck Coiling

As the crane lowers the line to the marine vessel's main deck, the deck crew guides the line into loops. The crane operator must adjust the lowering speed carefully to match the crew's pace, ensuring the line stays within its Minimum Bending Radius (MBR) to avoid damaging the internal layers.

3. Equipment Recovery & Demobilization

The tower operator lowers both DSRBs to the boat landing for storage. Using crane assistance, the crew disconnects all temporary high-pressure chicksans, cross-overs, and flanges, returning them to the vessel deck before casting off.

Lesson 13

HQDC Disconnect Protocols

The Hydraulic Quick Disconnect Coupling (HQDC) serves as the primary safety mechanism for flexible lines in marine operations. If a vessel loses position or severe weather threatens the platform, the lines must be disconnected cleanly to prevent structural damage.

18.1 Emergency Disconnect (ESD)

Triggered by sudden changes like dynamic positioning failures, anchor slippage, or severe weather surges.

  • The Vessel Master immediately alerts the Well Test Supervisor.
  • The well is shut in at the tower flow spool actuator and test vessel ESD valve.
  • Personnel quickly clear the stern and boat landing.
  • The Supervisor fires the HQDC from the control pod or main deck station.
  • The vessel releases its anchor lines and moves away, ensuring the lines are clear of the stern.
18.2 Controlled Disconnect

A planned operation used when weather changes are forecasted well in advance, or during scheduled moves.

  • The well is shut in using standard operating procedures.
  • The flexible line is fully depressurized through the choke manifold and burner boom.
  • The workspace is cleared of non-essential equipment.
  • The HQDC is actuated under stable, controlled conditions.
  • The vessel systematically disconnects and moves clear to assess the next steps.

Weather Contingency Warning (Level 4 & 5)

Do not attempt personnel transfers via gangway or basket if environmental conditions reach Level 4 or Level 5 weather contingencies. All transfers must wait until the flexible lines are either completely stored on deck or disconnected via HQDC, and the vessel has moved to a safe distance.

Lesson 14

Hose Recovery & Redressing

After an emergency disconnect or tactical pull-away, the lines must be safely recovered, inspected, and redressed before they can be placed back into service.

Physical Recovery & Realignment Sequence

20.1

Verify that all connected surface equipment and lines are completely depressurized to 0 psig.

20.2

Disconnect surface piping from the flow spool. Attach either the platform crane or the marine vessel crane line to the lifting slings on the Hose Hanger Adaptor (HHA).

20.3

Hoist the crane line slowly until the HHA clears the platform connections, keeping the line visible to the operator at all times.

20.4

Swing the crane toward the marine vessel deck. Lower the end of the line carefully, coiling it flat and securing it using structural deck tie-down slings.

20.5

Disconnect the Hydraulic Quick Disconnect Coupling (HQDC) mechanism. Clean, inspect, and redress the internal sealing components, then realign the hammer unions to prepare the lines for subsequent operations.
