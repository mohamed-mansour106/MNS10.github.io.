Atmospheric Storage Tank: Standard Operating Procedures

1. Introduction and Purpose of the Tank

Atmospheric storage tanks are essential in the oil and gas industry, particularly in remote areas, to support zero flaring by temporarily storing water, crude oil, and condensate [1]. These tanks allow operators to quantify daily production and avoid environmental damage caused by water dumping or oil flaring [1]. The primary purpose of this SOP is to provide guidelines for safe rig-up and operation without harming personnel, assets, or the environment [2].

A temporary storage system to avoid flaring and measure flow, used during well testing to store:

  • Crude Oil
  • Condensate
  • Produced Water
Goal: Zero Flaring + Zero Dumping. Protect the environment and measure production accurately.

2. Technical Context & Pressure Limit

This is NOT a pressure vessel. It is designed for atmospheric pressure.

  • Normal Operating Pressure: 0 psig
  • Max Allowed: ≈ 10–15 psig (extremely limited)

3. Critical Safety Checks

Before and during operation, several vital checks must be performed to ensure system integrity:

  • Vent lines must be routed to a flare burner or pit and kept clear of combustible hydrocarbon vapors [3].
  • Storage tank levels must not exceed 80% capacity to prevent liquid carry-over or foaming into the lines [4, 5].
  • Flame arrestors must be inspected every six hours and kept clear of debris to prevent flashbacks from the burner [3, 6].
  • Nitrogen stripping or blanketing should be used during discharge to avoid vacuum conditions [7, 8].

The 3 Critical Components

1. Vent Line

Allows gas to escape to flare. Must NEVER be blocked.

2. Flame Arrestor

Prevents external flames from traveling back into the tank.

3. Breather Valve

Controls air intake/exhaust to prevent collapse or rupture.

4. Hazard Prevention: Vacuum and Implosion

A vacuum is the absence of internal pressure, leaving the tank vulnerable to the constant force of atmospheric pressure (14.7 psia) [9]. If a full vacuum is created, the external force can cause the tank to collapse or implode, leading to total loss of the asset [9, 10]. Common causes of vacuum include closed valves in the vent line during fluid displacement, inoperable relief valves, or significant internal temperature drops [10, 11].

4. Major Hazards

⚠️ Fire & Explosion

Vapors are highly flammable. No phones, sparks, or static electricity allowed nearby.

⚠️ Tank Vacuum (Collapse)

If emptied too fast or vent is blocked, the 14.7 psi external pressure will crush the tank.

A tank can be "crushed" by the weight of the air around it if the internal pressure drops too low while emptying.

⚠️ Overfilling

Max Level: 80%. Prevents liquid carryover into the vent system.

5. Key Protection Devices

To mitigate the risks of rupture or implosion, the tanks utilize specialized protection equipment:

  • Breather Valves: These are direct-acting pressure/vacuum relief valves that allow the tank to "breathe" by satisfying internal pressure requirements during thermal changes or fluid pumping [12-14].
  • Blanketing Valves: These valves detect pressure decreases and introduce inert Nitrogen gas into the vapor space to maintain a safe internal pressure [15, 16].
  • Flame Arrestors: These prevent external ignition sources from causing a backfire into the vent line [17, 18].

5. Operation & Maintenance

Pre-Operation Checklist

  • Vent line connected to flare and free of liquid
  • Flame arrestor clean and inspected
  • Nitrogen (N₂) purge completed (Blanketing)
  • Tank level below 80%
  • No diesel stored

Venting Golden Rule

THE TANK MUST BREATHE.
No Breath = Explosion or Collapse.

6. Offloading Procedures

To Truck: Use transfer pump. Fill truck to 95% max to allow for thermal expansion.

To Burner: Ensure flame is active and air supply is steady before directing flow.

Atmospheric Storage Tank Operations & Safety

Physics

A sudden cold rain or temperature drop causes vapors inside to shrink. If the breather valve fails to let air in, the tank will implode (collapse) due to external atmospheric pressure.

Chemistry

It displaces Oxygen to keep the atmosphere below the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL), preventing internal combustion from static or sparks.

Fluid Dynamics

It causes sudden surges of gas and liquid, which can lead to instantaneous overpressure, vibrating the tank walls and potentially damaging the roof seal.

Maintenance

Cold crude can deposit wax on the mesh. A waxed arrestor acts as a blind flange, blocking the vent and causing pressure build-up during filling.

Operation

To prevent Liquid Carryover. If liquid enters the vent line, it can extinguish the flare or create a "Rain of Fire" hazard at the burner.

HSE

H2S is lethal at low concentrations. 50ppm is significantly above the PEL (Permissible Exposure Limit), requiring supplied air for safe access to the hatch.

Physics

High flow velocity through non-conductive hoses. Without proper bonding and grounding, this charge can spark and ignite hydrocarbon vapors.

Chemical

It is an additive that chemically reacts with H2S to convert it into a non-toxic liquid byproduct, making the fluid safe for truck transport.

Thermodynamics

To provide Ullage (expansion space). If the oil warms up during transit, it will expand; without space, the truck tank could rupture.

Safety

When a flame travels from the flare tip back through the vent line. The Flame Arrestor is the only barrier preventing this flame from entering the storage tank.

KEY CONCEPT

The tank faces Structural Implosion (Vacuum Collapse). As liquid leaves, if air cannot enter, the internal pressure drops and the external atmospheric pressure crushes the tank walls.

Remember: 14.7 psi of air pressure outside is a massive force against a non-pressure-rated wall.

SAFETY PROTOCOL

To displace Oxygen and maintain the vapor space below the Lower Explosive Limit (LEL).

By removing Oxygen, you eliminate the possibility of internal combustion even if a spark occurs.

OPERATIONAL LIMIT

To prevent liquid carryover into the vent and flare lines.

Filling beyond 80% risks pushing raw crude into the gas vent system, which can cause fires at the flare tip or block the flame arrestor.

MECHANICAL INTEGRITY

It creates a blocked vent condition, which leads to overpressure during tank filling.

A blocked arrestor effectively turns your atmospheric tank into a bomb as gas cannot escape while liquid is pumped in.
